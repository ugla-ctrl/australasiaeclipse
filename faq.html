FAQ

Answers to the questions eclipse chasers ask most — visas, kids, cameras, cruise ships, and why you really do need to be inside the path.

Saturday, 22 July 2028. The Moon’s shadow makes landfall on the Kimberley coast of Western Australia around 11:00 am AWST, crosses the continent through the Red Centre, reaches Sydney at 2:01 pm AEST, and sweeps New Zealand’s South Island around 4:15 pm NZST — all in under three hours. Yes — and this is the one thing veterans wish someone had told them. A 98% partial eclipse is not 98% of the experience; it’s a slightly dim afternoon. Only inside the ~230 km-wide path does the Sun fully vanish: stars appear at midday, the horizon glows 360° like sunset, the temperature drops, and the corona — the Sun’s atmosphere — hangs visibly in the sky. The difference between 99% and 100% is the difference between reading about it and being there.
It depends where you stand. The maximum is 5 minutes 10 seconds near the Kimberley — one of the longest totalities of the century. Devils Marbles in the NT gets ~4m 55s, Dubbo ~3m 49s, Sydney Harbour ~3m 48s, and New Zealand’s South Island 2½–3 minutes with a dramatic low winter Sun. Duration falls off toward the path edges, so aim for the central line.
The last total solar eclipse over Sydney was in 1857 — 171 years before this one. On eclipse day the entire harbour, CBD, Royal Botanic Garden and Opera House sit inside the path, with totality at 2:01 pm and the Sun 29° above the north-western sky. It will likely be the most-witnessed totality in Australian history.
Absolutely — with one rule. During all partial phases you must use ISO 12312-2 certified eclipse glasses (sunglasses are never enough). During totality itself, glasses come off — the corona is about as bright as the full Moon and completely safe to view with the naked eye. Kids handle it brilliantly; just rehearse the “glasses on / glasses off” moments with them beforehand. Order glasses early from reputable suppliers — they sell out continent-wide in the final weeks.
If this is your first totality, seriously consider not photographing it — the minutes go faster than you can imagine. If you must: you need a certified solar filter for every partial phase (for the lens, not just your eyes), a tripod, and a rehearsed plan for removing the filter at second contact. A phone on a small tripod shooting wide video captures the darkness, the horizon glow and the crowd reaction — often the best souvenir with the least fiddling.
July is the dry season across northern and central Australia — historical clear-sky odds run ~85–90% in the Kimberley and the Red Centre, among the best anywhere on the path. Outback NSW runs ~75%. Sydney in winter is closer to a coin flip (~50%). New Zealand’s South Island is the riskiest and the most beautiful: Fiordland is one of the wettest places on Earth, though a mountain vantage above the cloud deck can save the day.
Most visitors need only an electronic travel authority: Australia’s eVisitor / ETA and New Zealand’s NZeTA are typically granted online in minutes to days for US, UK, EU, Canadian, Japanese and many other passports. If you’re doing both countries (many chasers are), sort both before you fly. Check the official government sites for your nationality well ahead of eclipse season.
Yes — the southern winter, and it’s a feature. Outback days are mild (high teens to low 20s °C) with crisp, transparent skies; nights are cold and star-drenched. Sydney winter afternoons average ~17 °C. New Zealand’s South Island will be genuinely alpine-cold with a low golden Sun — pack layers and enjoy totality with snow on the Remarkables.
Yes. The path crosses the Indian Ocean before landfall and the Tasman Sea between Australia and New Zealand, and eclipse cruises position ships on the central line with the freedom to dodge cloud — the trick land-based chasers envy. The trade-off: a moving deck makes long-lens photography hard. For most people the naked-eye experience from a ship is spectacular.
Even under cloud, totality is eerie — midday night falls all the same. But mobility is the chaser’s superpower: watch the forecasts and be ready to drive along the path the night before. If you miss 2028 entirely, the next totals over this region are 13 July 2037 (Australia again — Sydney gets a second run) and 26 December 2038. The US waits until 2044–45.
